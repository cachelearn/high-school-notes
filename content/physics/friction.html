Friction

1. Types of Friction

TypeWhenFormula
Static friction $f_s$Body at rest, tendency to move$f_s \le \mu_s N$ (self-adjusting)
Limiting frictionJust about to move$f_{s,max} = \mu_s N$
Kinetic friction $f_k$Body already sliding$f_k = \mu_k N$ (constant)
Rolling frictionBody rollingMuch smaller than $f_k$

Always: $\mu_s > \mu_k > \mu_{rolling}$

Critical Point: Static friction is self-adjusting. It equals $\mu_s N$ only at the point of slipping. Before that it equals whatever force is needed to maintain equilibrium. This is the most commonly misunderstood fact in friction.

2. Laws of Friction

  • Friction $\propto$ Normal force $N$
  • Independent of area of contact
  • Kinetic friction independent of speed
  • Always opposes relative motion or tendency of it

3. Angle of Friction & Angle of Repose

$$\tan\lambda = \mu \qquad \text{(angle of friction)}$$

$$\tan\theta_r = \mu_s \qquad \text{(angle of repose = angle of friction)}$$

At $\theta > \theta_r$ the block slides; at $\theta = \theta_r$ it is on the verge of sliding.

5. Two-Block Slipping Condition

Block $m$ on top of block $M$, force $F$ on $m$, $\mu$ between them, smooth floor.

  1. Assume they move together: $a = F/(m+M)$
  2. Friction required on $m$: $f = ma$
  3. If $f \le \mu mg$ â†’ they move together âś“
  4. If $f > \mu mg$ â†’ they slip. Use $f = \mu mg$, write separate equations.
JEE Angle: This "check slipping" method is the most tested friction concept. Always assume together first, then verify.
Example: $m=2$ kg, $M=8$ kg, $\mu=0.3$, $F=15$ N.
If together: $a=1.5$ m/sÂ˛. Friction needed: $2\times1.5=3$ N. Max: $0.3\times2\times10=6$ N.
$3 < 6$ â†’ move together. $a = \mathbf{1.5\ \text{m/s}^2}$

6. Pulley System with Friction

$m_1$ on rough table ($\mu_k$), $m_2$ hanging. Moves if $m_2 > \mu_s m_1$.

$$a = \frac{m_2 g - \mu_k m_1 g}{m_1+m_2}, \qquad T = \frac{m_1 m_2 g(1+\mu_k)}{m_1+m_2}$$

7. Minimum Force to Move a Block

Force $F$ at angle $\theta$ above horizontal. As $\theta$ increases, $N$ decreases â†’ friction decreases, but horizontal component also decreases. Optimal angle:

$$\theta_{opt} = \tan^{-1}(\mu) = \lambda \qquad \text{(angle of friction)}$$

$$F_{min} = \frac{\mu mg}{\sqrt{1+\mu^2}}$$

8. Rolling Without Slipping

When a body rolls without slipping, the contact point has zero velocity relative to ground:

$$v_{CM} = r\omega$$

  • Friction is static (not kinetic) â€” it does no work in pure rolling.
  • Static friction provides torque for angular acceleration.
  • On a smooth surface: a sphere/cylinder can slide but not roll (no torque).
JEE Angle: Rolling without slipping condition $v=r\omega$ is critical. On rough incline: $a = \frac{g\sin\theta}{1 + I/mr^2}$ (from rotation chapter).

9. Ladder Problem

Ladder of mass $m$, length $L$, leaning at angle $\theta$ against smooth wall, rough floor ($\mu$).

Conditions for equilibrium: $\sum F = 0$ and $\sum \tau = 0$

Taking torques about base:

$$N_w \cdot L\sin\theta = mg\cdot\frac{L}{2}\cos\theta \implies N_w = \frac{mg}{2\tan\theta}$$

For not slipping: $f = N_w \le \mu N_f = \mu mg$

$$\tan\theta \ge \frac{1}{2\mu} \qquad \text{(minimum angle for ladder not to slip)}$$

10. Work Done by Friction & Heat

  • Kinetic friction always does negative work on the sliding body.
  • Static friction can do positive, negative, or zero work.
  • Heat generated $=$ friction force $\times$ relative displacement between surfaces:

$$Q = f_k \cdot s_{rel}$$

Example: Block slides 4 m, $\mu_k=0.4$, $m=2$ kg.
$Q = 0.4\times2\times10\times4 = \mathbf{32\ \text{J}}$
