Laws of Motion

1. Newton's First Law (Law of Inertia)

A body continues in its state of rest or uniform motion in a straight line unless acted upon by a net external force.

  • Inertia: Resistance of a body to change its state. Measured by mass.
  • No net force → no acceleration (but velocity can be non-zero).
  • Defines the concept of an inertial frame — a frame where this law holds.
JEE Angle: Used to identify pseudo forces in non-inertial frames, and to check if a system is in equilibrium.

2. Newton's Second Law

$$\vec{F}_{net} = m\vec{a} = \frac{d\vec{p}}{dt}$$

  • Force and acceleration are always in the same direction.
  • Apply component-wise: $F_x = ma_x$, $F_y = ma_y$.
  • SI unit of force: Newton (N) $=$ kg·m/s²
JEE Angle: Always draw a free body diagram (FBD). Apply $\sum F = ma$ along and perpendicular to motion separately.
Example: A 5 kg block is pushed by 20 N right, 8 N left.
$F_{net} = 12$ N → $a = 12/5 = \mathbf{2.4 \text{ m/s}^2}$

3. Newton's Third Law

For every action there is an equal and opposite reaction. Action-reaction pairs act on different bodies — they never cancel each other.

4. Free Body Diagram (FBD)

Isolate each body. Draw all forces acting on it: weight $mg$ downward, normal $N$ perpendicular to surface, tension $T$ along string away from body, applied forces and friction.

JEE Angle: Every multi-body problem needs separate FBDs. Write $F=ma$ for each body individually.

5. Standard Systems

Two Blocks in Contact

$$a = \frac{F}{m_1+m_2}, \qquad N_{contact} = \frac{m_2 F}{m_1+m_2}$$

Atwood's Machine

Masses $m_1 > m_2$ over a frictionless pulley:

$$a = \frac{(m_1-m_2)g}{m_1+m_2}, \qquad T = \frac{2m_1 m_2 g}{m_1+m_2}$$

Block on Frictionless Incline

$$a = g\sin\theta, \qquad N = mg\cos\theta$$

Block on Table + Hanging Mass

$m_1$ on table, $m_2$ hanging:

$$a = \frac{m_2 g}{m_1+m_2}, \qquad T = \frac{m_1 m_2 g}{m_1+m_2}$$

Example: Atwood: $m_1=6$ kg, $m_2=4$ kg, $g=10$.
$a = \frac{2\times10}{10} = \mathbf{2\ \text{m/s}^2}$,   $T = \frac{2\times6\times4\times10}{10} = \mathbf{48\ \text{N}}$

6. Wedge Problems

A block of mass $m$ on a frictionless wedge of angle $\theta$, wedge accelerating at $a_0$ on a smooth floor.

From ground frame (inertial):

$$N\sin\theta = ma_{block,x}, \qquad N\cos\theta - mg = 0 \text{ (if no vertical acc.)}$$

From wedge frame (non-inertial) — add pseudo force $ma_0$ backward on block:

Along incline: $mg\sin\theta - ma_0\cos\theta = ma_{rel}$

Perpendicular to incline: $N = mg\cos\theta + ma_0\sin\theta$

If block stays stationary relative to wedge (they move together):

$$a_0 = g\tan\theta, \qquad N = \frac{mg}{\cos\theta}$$

JEE Angle: Wedge problems are one of the most common JEE mechanics questions. Always choose the easier frame — usually the wedge frame with pseudo force.
Example: A wedge of angle $30°$ accelerates on a smooth floor. Find $a_0$ so the block doesn't slide.
$a_0 = g\tan30° = 10/\sqrt{3} \approx \mathbf{5.77\ \text{m/s}^2}$

7. Non-Inertial Frames & Pseudo Force

In a frame accelerating at $\vec{a}_0$, apply pseudo force $= -m\vec{a}_0$ on every body.

SituationApparent Weight
Lift accelerating up$m(g+a)$
Lift accelerating down$m(g-a)$
Free fall$0$
Constant velocity$mg$

8. System of Particles & Centre of Mass

For a system of particles with total mass $M$:

$$\vec{F}_{ext} = M\vec{a}_{CM}$$

The centre of mass accelerates as if all external forces act on it. Internal forces cancel in pairs.

Key Applications

  • Explosion: No external force → $\vec{p}_{total}$ conserved → $v_{CM}$ unchanged.
  • Recoil: Gun + bullet system: $0 = m_{bullet}v_{bullet} + m_{gun}v_{gun}$
  • Multiple blocks: $a_{CM} = F_{ext}/(m_1+m_2+\cdots)$
  • If no external force: CM moves at constant velocity (or stays at rest).
JEE Angle: Explosion and recoil problems are solved purely by momentum conservation. The CM doesn't move if there's no external force.
Example: A 4 kg gun fires a 0.05 kg bullet at 300 m/s. Find recoil velocity.
$0 = 0.05\times300 + 4\times v_{gun}$ → $v_{gun} = \mathbf{-3.75\ \text{m/s}}$

9. Variable Mass Systems

The safest approach for JEE — always start from first principles:

$F_{ext} = \frac{dp}{dt} = \frac{d(Mv)}{dt}$

The general form depends on whether mass is entering or leaving the system:

CaseEquation
Mass leaving (rocket, chain)$F_{ext} = M\dfrac{dv}{dt} - v_{rel}\dfrac{dM}{dt}$
Mass entering (sand on belt)$F_{ext} = M\dfrac{dv}{dt} + v_{rel}\dfrac{dM}{dt}$
Sign rule: $v_{rel}$ is the velocity of the mass being added/ejected relative to the system. Rather than memorising signs, derive case-by-case from $F = dp/dt$ — it's safer in exams.
SystemKey relation
Sand falling on conveyor beltForce to maintain speed: $F = v\dfrac{dm}{dt}$
Chain falling off tableAt length $x$ hanging: $F_{net} = \lambda xg$, where $\lambda$ = mass/length
Rocket equation$F_{thrust} = v_{exhaust}\dfrac{dM}{dt}$,   $v = u\ln(M_0/M)$
JEE Angle: Conveyor belt and chain problems appear regularly. For conveyor belt: energy lost to friction $=$ KE given to sand (not all of $Fv$ goes to KE).

10. Impulse & Momentum

$$\vec{J} = \vec{F}\cdot\Delta t = \Delta\vec{p}$$

  • Conservation of momentum: if $F_{ext}=0$, $\vec{p}$ is conserved.
  • Impulse = area under $F$-$t$ graph.
Example: 0.1 kg ball hits wall at 10 m/s, rebounds at 8 m/s.
$J = \Delta p = 0.1 \times (-8 - 10) = \mathbf{-1.8 \ \text{N s}}$

11. Constraint Relations

For inextensible strings, total string length is constant. Differentiate to relate accelerations.

  • Simple fixed pulley: $a_1 = a_2$
  • Movable pulley: $a_1 + a_2 = 2a_{pulley}$
Example: Movable pulley — block A and block B connected via a movable pulley. Pulley moves at $a_p$.
Constraint: $a_A + a_B = 2a_p$. If A is fixed: $a_B = 2a_p$. Tension in main rope $= 2T$ where $T$ is tension in each string segment.

12. Equilibrium & Lami's Theorem

Equilibrium: $\sum\vec{F} = 0$. For 3 concurrent forces:

$$\frac{F_1}{\sin\alpha} = \frac{F_2}{\sin\beta} = \frac{F_3}{\sin\gamma}$$
