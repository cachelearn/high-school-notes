Sequences & Series

1. Basic Definitions

  • Sequence: An ordered list of numbers following a rule.
  • Series: Sum of terms of a sequence.
  • $T_n$: General term (formula for the $n$th term).
  • $S_n$: Sum of first $n$ terms.

$$T_n = S_n - S_{n-1} \quad (n \ge 2), \qquad T_1 = S_1$$

Example: $S_n = 3n^2 + 2n$. Find $T_3$.
$T_3 = S_3 - S_2 = 33 - 16 = \mathbf{17}$.   Verify: $T_n = 6n-1$, $T_3 = 17$ ✓

2. Arithmetic Progression (AP)

Each term differs from the previous by constant common difference $d$.

$$a,\; a+d,\; a+2d,\; \ldots$$

ItemFormula
$n$th term$T_n = a + (n-1)d$
Sum of $n$ terms$S_n = \dfrac{n}{2}[2a + (n-1)d] = \dfrac{n}{2}(a+l)$
$n$th term from end$l - (n-1)d$

Properties

  • $a, b, c$ in AP $\Rightarrow 2b = a + c$
  • 3 terms in AP: take $a-d,\; a,\; a+d$ (sum $= 3a$)
  • 4 terms in AP: take $a-3d,\; a-d,\; a+d,\; a+3d$ (sum $= 4a$)
  • If $S_n$ is quadratic in $n$ with no constant term, the series is an AP
JEE Angle: The ratio $S_n/S_n'$ trick — to find $T_n/T_n'$, replace $n$ with $(2n-1)$ in the ratio.
Example: $S_{20} = 400$, $S_{40} = 1600$. Find $S_{10}$.
$2a+19d = 40$, $2a+39d=80$ → $d=2, a=1$ → $S_{10} = 5(2+18) = \mathbf{100}$

3. Geometric Progression (GP)

Each term is the previous multiplied by constant common ratio $r$.

$$a,\; ar,\; ar^2,\; ar^3,\; \ldots$$

ItemFormula
$n$th term$T_n = ar^{n-1}$
Sum ($r \ne 1$)$S_n = \dfrac{a(r^n - 1)}{r - 1}$
Sum ($r = 1$)$S_n = na$
Sum to infinity$S_\infty = \dfrac{a}{1-r}$,   only when $-1 < r < 1$

Properties

  • $a, b, c$ in GP $\Rightarrow b^2 = ac$
  • 3 terms in GP: take $a/r,\; a,\; ar$ (product $= a^3$)
  • $\log$ of GP terms forms an AP
Example: Express $0.\overline{23}$ as a fraction.
$a = 23/100$, $r = 1/100$ → $S_\infty = \dfrac{23/100}{1-1/100} = \mathbf{\dfrac{23}{99}}$

4. Harmonic Progression (HP)

A sequence is in HP if the reciprocals of its terms form an AP.

$$T_n^{HP} = \frac{1}{a + (n-1)d} \qquad \text{(where } a, d \text{ are from the reciprocal AP)}$$

Harmonic Mean of $a$ and $b$: $\; HM = \dfrac{2ab}{a+b}$

JEE Angle: No direct sum formula — always convert to AP by taking reciprocals.
Example: 4th term of HP: $1/2, 1/5, 1/8, \ldots$
Reciprocals: $2, 5, 8, \ldots$ (AP, $d=3$) → $T_4 = 2+9 = 11$ → HP term $= \mathbf{1/11}$

5. AM, GM, HM — The Golden Inequality

For two positive numbers $a$ and $b$:

$$AM = \frac{a+b}{2}, \qquad GM = \sqrt{ab}, \qquad HM = \frac{2ab}{a+b}$$

$$AM \;\ge\; GM \;\ge\; HM$$

Equality holds if and only if $a = b$. Also: $AM \times HM = GM^2$.

JEE Angle: Find min/max of expressions, or find two numbers given AM and GM.
Example: AM $= 5$, GM $= 4$. Find the numbers.
$a+b=10$, $ab=16$ → $(a-b)^2 = 100-64 = 36$ → $a=\mathbf{8}, b=\mathbf{2}$
Min of $x + 1/x$ for $x > 0$: By AM-GM, $\;\dfrac{x+1/x}{2} \ge 1$ → min $= \mathbf{2}$ at $x=1$

6. Arithmetic-Geometric Progression (AGP)

Terms are products of AP and GP terms: $\;a,\;(a+d)r,\;(a+2d)r^2,\;\ldots$

Never memorise the formula — derive every time:
Write $S$, then write $rS$ (shifted), subtract $S - rS$, the result telescopes to a GP + one extra term. Solve.
Example: $S = 1 + 2x + 3x^2 + \cdots$ (infinite, $|x|<1$)
$S - xS = 1 + x + x^2 + \cdots = \dfrac{1}{1-x}$
$\Rightarrow S = \dfrac{1}{(1-x)^2}$

7. Special Series — Sum of Powers

$$\sum_{r=1}^n 1 = n \qquad \sum_{r=1}^n r = \frac{n(n+1)}{2}$$

$$\sum_{r=1}^n r^2 = \frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6} \qquad \sum_{r=1}^n r^3 = \left[\frac{n(n+1)}{2}\right]^2$$

$\sum r^3 = \left(\sum r\right)^2$ — this identity is a JEE favourite.
  • Sum of first $n$ odd numbers $= n^2$
  • Sum of first $n$ even numbers $= n(n+1)$
Example: $1^2 + 2^2 + \cdots + 10^2 = \dfrac{10 \times 11 \times 21}{6} = \mathbf{385}$

8. Method of Differences

Used when $T_n$ is not obvious but the differences of consecutive terms form an AP or GP.

  1. Write $T_1, T_2, T_3, \ldots$ and compute first differences.
  2. If differences are in AP/GP, identify $T_n$ by summing the differences.
  3. Then find $S_n = \sum T_n$ using standard formulas.
Example: $1, 3, 7, 13, 21, \ldots$
Differences: $2, 4, 6, 8, \ldots$ (AP) → $T_n = 1 + \sum_{k=1}^{n-1} 2k = n^2 - n + 1$

9. Telescoping Series

Write $T_n = f(n) - f(n+1)$ so most terms cancel in the sum.

PatternSplit
$\dfrac{1}{n(n+1)}$$\dfrac{1}{n} - \dfrac{1}{n+1}$
$\dfrac{1}{(2n-1)(2n+1)}$$\dfrac{1}{2}\!\left(\dfrac{1}{2n-1} - \dfrac{1}{2n+1}\right)$
JEE Angle: Use partial fractions to split $T_n$, then telescope. Very standard technique.
Example: $\dfrac{1}{1\cdot2} + \dfrac{1}{2\cdot3} + \cdots$ to $n$ terms.
$T_n = \dfrac{1}{n} - \dfrac{1}{n+1}$ → telescopes → $S = 1 - \dfrac{1}{n+1} = \mathbf{\dfrac{n}{n+1}}$

10. $V_n$ Method

When $T_n$ is a product of $k$ terms in AP with common difference $d$:

$$S_n = \frac{1}{(k+1)\,d}\left[V_{n+1} - V_1\right]$$

where $V_r$ is the product of $k+1$ consecutive AP terms starting at $T_r$.

Example: $\sum \dfrac{1}{n(n+1)(n+2)}$ → $k=3$, $d=1$
$S_n = \dfrac{1}{2}\!\left[\dfrac{1}{1\cdot2} - \dfrac{1}{(n+1)(n+2)}\right]$

11. Infinite GP & Convergence

$S_\infty = \dfrac{a}{1-r}$   only when $-1 < r < 1$. For $|r| \ge 1$, the series diverges.

Recurring Decimals

FormFraction
$0.\overline{a}$$a/9$
$0.a\overline{b}$$(ab - a)/90$
$0.\overline{ab}$$ab/99$
Example: Sum of infinite GP is 3, sum of squares is also 3. Find the GP.
$a/(1-r)=3$ and $a^2/(1-r^2)=3$ → $r=1/2$, $a=3/2$
GP: $\mathbf{3/2,\; 3/4,\; 3/8,\; \ldots}$

12. Insertion of Means

TypeFormula for $d$ or $r$Useful Result
$n$ AM's between $a$ and $b$$d = \dfrac{b-a}{n+1}$Sum of $n$ AM's $= n\cdot\dfrac{a+b}{2}$
$n$ GM's between $a$ and $b$$r = \left(\dfrac{b}{a}\right)^{1/(n+1)}$Product of $n$ GM's $= (ab)^{n/2}$
$n$ HM's between $a$ and $b$Take reciprocals → insert $n$ AM's → take reciprocals back
Example: Insert 3 AM's between 3 and 19.
$d = (19-3)/4 = 4$ → Means: $\mathbf{7, 11, 15}$

13. Properties & Key Tricks

Ratio of Sums → Ratio of nth Terms

If $S_n/S_n' = f(n)/g(n)$, then $T_n/T_n' = f(2n-1)/g(2n-1)$.

Example: $S_n/S_n' = (7n+1):(4n+27)$. Find $T_{11}/T_{11}'$.
Replace $n$ with $2(11)-1=21$ → $(148):(111) = \mathbf{4:3}$

Other Tricks

  • $S_n = An^2 + Bn$ (no constant) → series is AP, $T_n = A(2n-1)+B$
  • $S_n = Ar^n$ (exponential) → series is GP
  • Three numbers in AP and GP simultaneously → they must all be equal
  • $\log$ of GP terms forms AP; $\text{antilog}$ of AP terms forms GP

14. Miscellaneous

Mixed Series

Break $\sum(ar^2 + br + c)$ into $a\sum r^2 + b\sum r + c\sum 1$ and apply standard formulas.

Example: $\sum_{r=1}^n r(r+1) = \sum r^2 + \sum r = \dfrac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6} + \dfrac{n(n+1)}{2} = \dfrac{n(n+1)(n+2)}{3}$

Geometric Series with Negative Ratio

$1 - x + x^2 - x^3 + \cdots = \dfrac{1}{1+x}$   for $|x|<1$

16. Differentiation & Integration of Series

Base (Geometric Series): $\;\dfrac{1}{1-x} = 1 + x + x^2 + \cdots, \quad |x|<1$

Differentiate once: $\;\dfrac{1}{(1-x)^2} = 1 + 2x + 3x^2 + \cdots = \sum_{r=1}^\infty r\,x^{r-1}$

Multiply by $x$: $\;\dfrac{x}{(1-x)^2} = \sum_{r=1}^\infty r\,x^r$

Integrate base: $\;-\ln(1-x) = x + \dfrac{x^2}{2} + \dfrac{x^3}{3} + \cdots$

$\;\ln(1+x) = x - \dfrac{x^2}{2} + \dfrac{x^3}{3} - \cdots$

Strategy: If $T_n$ has $r$ or $r^2$ → differentiate the GP sum. If $T_n$ has $1/r$ → integrate. Substitute $x = 1/2$, $1/3$, etc. to evaluate.
Example: $S = \dfrac{1}{2} + \dfrac{2}{4} + \dfrac{3}{8} + \cdots = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \dfrac{n}{2^n}$
$= \sum n\,x^n$ at $x=\tfrac{1}{2}$ $= \dfrac{x}{(1-x)^2}\Big|_{x=1/2} = \dfrac{1/2}{1/4} = \mathbf{2}$
